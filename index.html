Research

Revealing the CGM with an Einstein Cross

We use an Einstein Cross system to study the CGM of a star-forming galaxy at z = 1.27. The four lensed images of a background galaxy probe different regions of the halo, revealing Mg II absorption that traces the spatial and kinematic structure of the enriched gas on kpc scales. This provides new constraints on the CGM’s origin and allows a direct comparison with traditional quasar absorption-line studies.


Kinematic characterization of the cool intragroup medium

The CGM links galaxies to their large-scale environment, regulating both accretion and feedback. Although most galaxies reside in groups, the physical and kinematic properties of the CGM in these environments remain poorly constrained. We present the first spatially resolved characterization of the cool intragroup medium (IGrM) in a spectroscopically confirmed galaxy group at z ≃ 1.167, mapping the spatial distribution and kinematics of diffuse, metal-enriched gas.

    About Me

    Hi everyone!

    I am Fernanda, but everyone calls me Fer. I am a Chilean astronomer currently pursuing a Master's degree in Astrophysics at Pontificia Universidad Católica de Chile, where I also completed my Bachelor's degree.

    During my studies, I developed a strong interest in extragalactic astronomy. My main research focuses on galaxy evolution across cosmic time and on studying the circumgalactic medium (CGM) and the baryon cycle. That's why I joined the ARCTOMO team, where we use gravitational lensing as a tool to explore the CGM!

    In connection with this work, I have participated in several conferences and workshops — presenting my research, helping with their organization, taking photos, and welcoming attendees.

    Outreach

    Besides astronomy research, I have gained experience in teaching and science communication. I have worked as a teaching and grading assistant for several courses at my university. Two of these courses — Data Analysis and Astronomy Laboratory — allowed me to refine my programming skills in Python and to work with software such as SExtractor and TOPCAT , as students engaged directly with images, large databases, and statistics applied to astronomy in cloud-based environments.

    I have also organized workshops and help sessions for students in my faculty, focusing on programming tools such as Python and LaTeX.

    Additionally, I participated as an assistant at the university’s observatory in Santa Martina , where I gained experience in communicating science in an accessible way to students from non-scientific backgrounds, as well as developing skills in outreach and public speaking.
